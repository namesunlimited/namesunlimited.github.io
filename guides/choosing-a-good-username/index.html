How to Pick a Username That's Actually Available

Names Unlimited Guides · 4 min read

The most obvious usernames — first name plus last name, first name plus birth year — are exactly the ones everyone else tries first. Widening your options before you start checking availability saves a lot of repeated searching, and it's usually faster than it sounds once you have a batch of realistic options to react to instead of a blank text field.

Why the obvious options are usually taken

On any platform with millions of users, the simplest, most predictable username formats get claimed early. If your instinct is "firstname_lastname" or "firstname123", assume it's already gone and have a backup plan before you start. This isn't a reflection of anything unusual about your name — it's simple math, since a handful of common formats get tried by a huge share of new signups every single day.

Generate more options than you need

Rather than testing one username at a time, generate a batch of ten to twenty varied options up front — mixing formats like dotted (first.last), underscored (first_last), and shortened (first + last initial) — and check them as a group. This turns a frustrating back-and-forth into a quick checklist.

Decide how much you want it to reveal

Some usernames are meant to be recognizably you (professional platforms, portfolio sites); others are meant to be more private (gaming, forums, anonymous accounts). Decide which category applies before generating options — it changes whether you want your real name pattern at all, or something further removed from your identity.

Keep it consistent if you'll use it across platforms

If you want the same handle on multiple sites, check availability everywhere before settling on one — a username that's free on one platform and taken on another creates a fragmented online presence that's harder for people to find or for you to manage.

Avoid patterns that age badly

A birth year, a school graduation year, or a reference to something time-bound can feel dated within a few years. Formats built from just a name, or a name plus a small varying number, tend to hold up better over time.

What to do when your best options are all taken

Before adding random numbers, try small deliberate variations first: swapping the order (lastfirst instead of firstlast), using a middle initial, or trying a shortened version of the last name. These tend to read as more intentional than appending "2024" or a long string of digits, and they're often still available even on large platforms.

Usernames for professional versus casual contexts

A username meant to appear on a resume, portfolio site, or professional network benefits from being close to your real name and easy to say aloud in conversation. A gaming or casual-forum handle has much more room to be playful, since the goal there is usually memorability within a community rather than professional recognisability. Deciding which mode you're in before generating options narrows the list fast.

Frequently asked questions

Is it better to use the same username everywhere? It's convenient for people to find you, but not essential — many people intentionally keep professional and casual usernames separate for privacy or tone reasons.

Should a username include numbers? Only if needed for availability. A clean, number-free username generally looks more polished, but a short, non-date-based number is a reasonable fallback.

How often do people change usernames? Less often than they think they will — a username picked with some care up front usually gets kept for years, which is a good reason to spend a few extra minutes choosing rather than grabbing the first available option.

A simple checklist before you commit

Before finalising a username, it helps to run through a short mental checklist: does it still make sense read aloud to someone else, does it avoid a personal detail you'd rather keep private, is it free of anything that could age awkwardly in five years, and have you actually confirmed it's available on the platform you're signing up to rather than assuming. Running a shortlist of three or four generated options through that checklist, instead of committing to the very first one that feels close enough, is usually the difference between a username you're still happy with years later and one you quietly regret.

The underlying goal is simple even when the search feels tedious: a username that's easy for you to type without thinking, easy for someone else to read back correctly, and comfortable to see attached to your name for as long as you keep the account. Starting from a batch of realistic, name-based options rather than random characters makes it much easier to hit all three at once.

Finally, resist the urge to treat this as a one-shot decision made under pressure at the exact moment a signup form is open. Spending five minutes beforehand generating and shortlisting a handful of options — before you're staring at an "already taken" error message — tends to produce a much better result than reacting in the moment with whatever variation comes to mind first.

Treat a username the way you'd treat any small, long-lived decision: worth a few minutes of care up front, not worth agonising over for hours. A shortlist of solid options beats a single "perfect" one that turns out to be taken anyway.
