How to Choose a Name for a Fictional Character

Names Unlimited Guides · 6 min read

Naming a character can eat up more time than the scene you were actually trying to write. The good news is that most of the decision comes down to a handful of practical checks rather than pure inspiration — once you know what to look for, picking a name gets much faster.

Start with what the name needs to do

Before generating options, decide what job the name is doing. A protagonist's name usually needs to be easy to read hundreds of times without friction. A minor character's name mostly needs to not be confused with anyone else's. A villain's name often benefits from a harder sound (sharp consonants, closed syllables) while a warm side-character can lean toward softer, more open sounds. None of this is a hard rule, but it's a useful filter when you're staring at a generated list of forty names.

Avoid collisions across your cast

Readers skim. If two major characters have names that start with the same letter, share a syllable count, or rhyme, readers will mix them up — especially in dialogue-heavy scenes without dialogue tags on every line. A quick way to check: list your main cast's names in a column and scan for any that look similar at a glance.

Match the name to setting and era

A name that reads as contemporary can pull a reader out of a historical or fantasy setting, and vice versa. If your story is set in a specific decade or culture, it's worth cross-checking a shortlist against real naming trends from that period rather than relying on what simply "sounds right" — names that feel timeless to us today were sometimes rare or nonexistent in the era being depicted.

Say it out loud, more than once

Names that look fine silently on the page sometimes trip on the tongue, especially in combination with a title, nickname, or another character's name in the same sentence ("Sarah said to Sara..."). Read your shortlist aloud in a sentence, not just on its own.

Use a generator to get unstuck, not to finish the job

The fastest workflow is usually: generate a large batch, skim for anything that has the right "shape" for the character, then adjust manually — swap a letter, shorten it, or pair it with a different surname from the same batch. Treat generated names as raw material rather than a final answer, and you'll move through a full cast list far faster than brainstorming each one individually.

Keep a running list as you write

For longer projects, keep a simple document of every named character, even minor ones, so you can check new names against it as the manuscript grows. It's much easier to catch a naming collision on page 40 than to discover it during a final read-through on page 300.

Common naming mistakes to avoid

A few patterns come up often enough to call out directly. Overly symbolic names — where the meaning is so on-the-nose it reads as a label rather than a person ("Mr. Grim" for the villain) — tend to undercut a story's credibility unless the tone is deliberately playful. Names that are technically pronounceable but visually dense with apostrophes or silent letters slow readers down every single time they appear, which adds up across a full manuscript. And reusing a name from a well-known existing work, even unintentionally, can create a distracting association you didn't intend.

Frequently asked questions

How many names should I generate before choosing one? Somewhere between twenty and fifty is usually enough to spot two or three genuinely strong options — fewer than that and you may be settling too early, more than that and the extra volume rarely changes the outcome.

Should main characters and minor characters be named differently? It helps to spend more deliberate time on names your reader will see hundreds of times, while minor characters can often be assigned quickly from a generated batch since the bar is mainly "distinct and easy to read."

Is it okay to rename a character partway through a draft? Yes — many writers do this once a character's voice solidifies and the placeholder name stops fitting. A simple find-and-replace pass at the end of a draft is far less disruptive than getting stuck early trying to find the "perfect" name before writing a word.

A quick workflow to take away

Put together, a fast and reliable process looks like this: generate a large batch filtered to the right gender and era, skim for names with the right "shape" for each character's role, cross-check the shortlist against your existing cast for collisions, read the finalists aloud in a sentence, then commit and move on. Treat the whole exercise as a five-minute checkpoint rather than a creative decision to agonise over — the story itself will tell you fast enough if a name isn't working, and it's always fixable later with a find-and-replace pass.

Ultimately, the names readers remember are rarely the ones that took longest to choose — they're the ones attached to characters who did memorable things. A solid, unobtrusive name paired with strong characterisation will always outperform a "perfect" name on a flat character, so it's worth keeping the naming step proportionate to everything else on your to-do list for the draft.
