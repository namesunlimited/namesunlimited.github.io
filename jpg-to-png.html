JPG vs PNG

What Is JPG and Why Convert It to PNG?

JPG (also written JPEG, short for Joint Photographic Experts Group) is the most common photo format in the world. Cameras, phones, and most websites default to it because it uses lossy compression — it throws away small amounts of image data every time the file is saved, which keeps file sizes small but slowly chips away at quality with repeated edits.

PNG (Portable Network Graphics) takes the opposite approach. It is a lossless format, meaning it stores every pixel exactly as it is, with no data thrown away and no compression artifacts. That makes PNG the format of choice the moment your image needs to look perfect, support transparency, or survive multiple rounds of editing without degrading.

Converting a JPG to PNG doesn't undo compression damage a photo already has, but it locks in the image exactly as it looks right now, so nothing further is lost from this point forward. This is the single biggest reason designers, developers, and photographers convert JPG files to PNG before they do any serious editing.

Quick way to think about it: JPG is for taking and sharing photos. PNG is for editing, designing, and publishing graphics that need to stay sharp.
Why Convert

The Real Reasons People Convert JPG to PNG

1. Stop Quality Loss During Editing

Every time you open a JPG in an editor and hit save, the compression runs again. Do that ten times across a project and the photo visibly degrades — soft edges, color banding, blotchy artifacts around text and lines. Converting to PNG first means every future save preserves the image pixel-for-pixel, no matter how many rounds of editing it goes through.

2. Unlock Transparency

JPG cannot store transparent pixels. Every single pixel in a JPG must have a solid color, even the background. PNG supports a full alpha channel, meaning parts of the image can be fully transparent, fully opaque, or anywhere in between. If you're planning to remove a background, layer a logo over another design, or build an icon that needs to sit cleanly on any color, you need a PNG base to work from.

3. Get Clean Text, Lines, and Solid Colors

JPG compression is tuned for photographs — it hides its losses well in busy, organic textures like skin, sky, and foliage. It performs badly on flat colors, sharp edges, and text, producing a blurry halo or "ringing" effect around letters and hard lines. Screenshots, scanned documents, logos, and UI mockups all look noticeably sharper and cleaner once converted to PNG.

4. Prepare for Print and Professional Design Software

Print shops, embroidery digitizers, and many design tools (T-shirt printing, sublimation, engraving) ask for PNG specifically because it preserves exact color values and supports transparent layers, which is essential for placing artwork onto different colored materials.

5. Archive Without Future Degradation

If a photo matters enough to keep for years, PNG is the safer long-term container. Re-saving a PNG never re-compresses it lossily, so the archived copy looks in ten years exactly as it looks today.

Heads Up

A Common Misconception About Transparency

Converting a JPG to PNG does not automatically make the background transparent. The PNG format is capable of transparency, but a straight JPG-to-PNG conversion just repackages the same solid background into a lossless container — nothing is removed.

To actually get a transparent background, you need to:

  1. Convert your JPG to PNG first (this gives you a lossless file that's safe to edit).
  2. Use a background remover or editing tool to erase the background and turn those pixels transparent.
  3. Save the result as PNG so the transparent areas are preserved.

If background removal is what you're actually after, look for a dedicated background remover tool — a plain format conversion is only step one.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Does converting JPG to PNG improve image quality? ▾
No conversion can restore detail that JPG compression already discarded. What it does is stop further quality loss — from this point forward, every save of the PNG is pixel-perfect. If the original JPG was heavily compressed or blurry, converting it to PNG keeps it exactly that blurry, just without adding more damage on top.
Why did my PNG file get bigger after converting? ▾
This is expected and not a bug. JPG uses lossy compression to keep files small; PNG uses lossless compression, which preserves every pixel but can't shrink the file nearly as much. A 500 KB JPG can easily become a 3–5 MB PNG. If file size matters more than transparency or lossless quality, keep the JPG.
Will my image get a transparent background automatically? ▾
No. A JPG never contains transparency data, so converting it to PNG just carries over the same solid background in a lossless container. You need a separate background-removal step afterward if you want a transparent PNG.
Can I convert more than one JPG at a time? ▾

What other formats can I convert to PNG here? ▾

Why do some of my old photos look blocky even after converting to PNG? ▾
That blockiness comes from JPG compression artifacts that were baked into the image before you converted it. PNG will preserve that blockiness exactly as it is, since PNG cannot add back detail that JPG compression already removed. To avoid this in the future, convert important photos to PNG the first time you save them, before repeated JPG re-saves compound the damage.
